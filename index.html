Air-cooled, so this stage adds no water — it trades the evaporation it avoids for compressor electricity. It sits in series after the dry + adiabatic coolers and only trims the residual gap to FWS, so its load is the shortfall fraction of the total heat. Capacity is sized to the peak (design-day) trim hour. A higher FWS (IceBrick) shrinks both the capacity you must install and the energy it burns — often to zero in dry climates.

Hot-dry markets (Phoenix, Las Vegas, Dallas) show the largest absolute savings because they spend the most hours in adiabatic mode today; cool-maritime markets (Seattle, Portland) already run mostly dry, so the lever is small in absolute terms even when the percentage is high.

Method & assumptions

Cooling logic. For each hour the dry cooler can deliver FWS = dry-bulb + approach. When dry-bulb rises above (FWS − approach), adiabatic pre-cooling sprays water to drop inlet air toward the wet-bulb, limited by the adiabatic effectiveness ε applied to the (dry-bulb − wet-bulb) gap. If full spray still can't reach FWS, the hour is flagged as chiller-trim and water is held at the adiabatic maximum.

Water. Evaporated water = Qrej × ΔTprecool / (ΔTair-coil × hfg), hfg = 2257 kJ/kg, times the drift/makeup factor. Qrej = IT load × heat-capture fraction. WUE = annual water ÷ annual IT energy.

Weather. Hourly dry-bulb and dewpoint are synthesized from monthly climate normals (mean, daily range, mean dewpoint) with a seeded diurnal + synoptic model; wet-bulb via the Stull approximation. This is a deterministic screening generator — for a bankable number, swap in real TMY3 / AMY 8760 files per site. The IceBrick case models only the FWS uplift enabled by buffering slow thermal transients; it does not yet credit chiller-energy or PUE effects, which would add to the savings.
